I recently purchased "When Breath Becomes Air" by Paul Kalanithi on Google Books before a return flight from New Zealand, and read it in a couple days.  It is a short story about Paul's career as a neurosurgeon, and his subsequent battle with cancer.  

I liked this book because it gives a circumspect view of mortality, both from the 3rd-person medical understanding, and the 1st-person experiential understanding.  Knowing what death is like before facing it yourself allows you to live life more deliberately, and to arrive at your own end peacefully.  It is an uncomfortable and sad look into the reality that each and every one of us will face.  I could not stop reading - it is the first book I have finished to completion in the past couple years.  

# Summary
Paul wrote the story as he died from lung cancer, so it is to-the-point and direct.  His perspective is specifically valuable because he had the capability and aspirations to become a writer, but was pulled into medicine by his desire to understand what makes a life meaningful.  He was a constant philosopher, wondering at hard questions and striving to be the best, putting himself in situations which allowed organic understanding of life's hardest questions.  He realized, as a doctor, he could provide direct value to patients grappling with life's final outcome - their own mortality.  

As a doctor he would take responsibility for the patient's mortality, hiding information as necessary and providing only the level of accuracy that he could be sure of.  He would never say "you have 6 months to live," after all who was he to say?  Instead, he would say that many people tend to live several years.  In framing the survival curve, he provided hope, comfort, and stability to people on the precipice of falling apart.

Paul contracted lung cancer just as he approached the end of his medical residency, as a shoe-in to a highly coveted neuroscience role at Stanford, combining practice with research.  After striving towards his goal for decades, his life was abruptly halted by the very thing he specialized in.  As the symptoms manifested in Paul, he gained a different understanding and appreciation for his patients' experience.  

# Thoughts
It is valuable to experience death before it comes for you because this allows you to "being with the end in mind."  This reality of life is often ignored, as is is uncomfortable and hard to handle.  What is the point of living if we are going to die?  What constitutes a meaningful life during our time here?  The earlier you can ask these questions, the more likely you are to be fulfilled.  

Of course, it is possible at any time that, like Paul, your life may be cut short and your search for meaning may come up with no results.  Life is not fair, and this is another aspect that the book drives home.  There is no great arbiter, who doles out reward for input.  Life is random, and diseases - especially cancer - is cruel and unfair.  The coping mechanisms and mindsets that result are to be understood and accounted for.  Dying of cancer is painful and unsightly.  Paul's body crashed, with tumors eventually limiting his ability to breath to the point where he was on a breather and unable to move.  Paul certainly did not deserve this.  He faced his problems with grace nonetheless, and was able to call an audible and provide value to the world in a unique way - by sharing his experience with the world.  

I am sure you have people in your life that you are close to that have experienced or are experiencing cancer.  Personally this book gave me greater sympathy with their struggles, and allowed me to put myself in their shoes.  It is a scary and dark thing.  I hope now I can be more present and available to these people.  I also hope I am spurred to action, to not rest on my laurels.  We do not have unlimited time.  We may not even have as much time as we think.  Be grateful for each day.  Do what you want and provide value however you can.  In the end, make your life worth living.  It is perhaps the most important thing.
